# Community

*The communal place, how we govern ourselves, and how we sustain the work.*

---

## The Communal Place

The physical heart of every L·A·B community is its gathering space —
a building that embodies the values of the religion in its very construction.

### Setting & Construction

The communal place is above all a place of meeting — shaped by the environment in which the Labber community lives and the aesthetic it has chosen together.

It may be nestled in nature or situated in the heart of a city. It may be built from wood and stone, or from steel and reclaimed industrial material. It may feel ancient or radically contemporary — a forest clearing, a converted warehouse, a basement club space, a rooftop, or something else entirely. What matters is that it fits the community's world, invites gathering, and does not create discomfort.

Materials may be locally sourced, repurposed, upcycled, or recycled — with sustainability considered as a standing value, not a fixed prescription for style.

The communal place should feel unmistakably like *this* community's space — not a template imposed from outside.

### Design Principles Ideas

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Environmental sustainability** | Passive solar design, renewable energy, composting, recycling |
| **Accessibility** | Wheelchair accessible; welcoming to all physical abilities and backgrounds |
| **Community first** | Seating arranged in circles; no stage, no hierarchy of space |
| **Flexibility** | Space reconfigures for different events, seasons, and needs |
| **Contextual integration** | Incorporating the surrounding world — green gardens, street art, acoustic club treatments, or digital displays |
| **Conscious sourcing** | Intentional material choices — whether locally sourced wood, recycled industrial steel, or repurposed urban fabric |
| **Safety** | Designed with the wellbeing of all members as the primary concern |

### Infrastructure ideas

- **Context-specific features** — Green roofs for eco-buildings; acoustic treatments for club spaces; digital spaces for tech-centric gatherings
- **Conscious energy use** — Solar panels, optimized power grids, or repurposed industrial energy
- **Resource systems** — Rainwater collection, shared urban utilities, or low-draw fixtures
- **Communal kitchen and dining area** — for weekly meals and daily gathering
- **Quiet spaces** — for meditation, reflection, and solitude
- **Community art** — walls featuring work made by members; the space as a living gallery

## Governance

L·A·B communities are governed by the members who comprise them.
There is no central authority, no permanent hierarchy, no external institution.

### Principles

**1. Participatory decision-making**
Every member has a voice. Decisions that affect the community are made with the community.
Processes are designed to ensure all voices — not only the loudest — are heard.
The goal is always the consensus, not compromise.

**2. Transparency and accountability**
Leaders report to the community, not the other way around.
Decision-making is open; reasoning is shared; outcomes are reviewed together.
Decisions should be made only after pre-discussing them with the ones who will be the recipients of changes.

**3. Inclusivity and equity**
The governance process itself must model the values it protects.
Diversity of background, experience, and perspective is actively sought, not tolerated.

**4. Environmental sustainability**
Governance decisions include environmental impact as a standing consideration —
not an afterthought.

**5. Service and altruism**
Leadership in L·A·B is a form of service. Those who lead do so for the community's benefit,
not their own advancement.
Regular rotation of leadership amongst the ones willing to help the governing is encouraged.

## Finance

L·A·B has no mandatory tithes, no membership fees, no financial requirements of any kind.

Financial support for the community comes entirely from **voluntary contributions**
by members who share its values and wish to sustain its work.

Funds raised are used for:
- Maintenance and sustainability of communal spaces
- Environmental care and conservation projects
- Education and community programming
- Support for members in need

Financial decisions are made transparently, in accordance with the governance principles above.

## Community Representatives and Roles ideas

Members of the community may take on representative roles — not as titles of authority,
but as callings toward a particular kind of service or as an acknowledgment of their acts.

| Role | Focus |
|---|---|
| L·A·B Mediator | Conflict resolution and community cohesion |
| L·A·B Caregiver | Compassion and care for those who are struggling |
| L·A·B Advocate | Equity, challenge of injustice, and advocacy |
| L·A·B Ambassador | Active outreach and community-building |
| L·A·B Mentor | Mentorship, guidance, and spiritual support |
| L·A·B Educator | Universal teaching, shared learning, and accountability practices |
| L·A·B Reconciler | Facilitating reconciliation and Forgiveness Circles |
| L·A·B Organizer | Planning community life, meals, and communal events |
| L·A·B Pattern Weaver | Excellent pattern embodiment and sharing the craft |
